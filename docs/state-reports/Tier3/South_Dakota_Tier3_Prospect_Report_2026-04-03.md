## QUESTIONS FOR BRETT

1. **Falcon Plastics scale and multi-shore complexity:** Falcon operates 152+ presses across 7 facilities in 4 countries (U.S., Mexico, China, plus Tigard OR). They're aggressively pursuing 33–50% lights-out manufacturing by 2030 with cobots and 6-axis robots. At this scale and geographic dispersion, is the Alliance value proposition centered on cross-facility data integration and process standardization, or would Aquila's platform approach them differently than a single-site molder?

2. **Falcon's missing certifications despite market exposure:** Falcon molds for automotive, medical, and aerospace-adjacent customers but holds only ISO 9001:2015 — no IATF 16949, AS9100, or ISO 13485. Is this a common pattern Brett has seen where successful custom molders defer advanced certifications, and does it represent a quality engineering engagement opportunity?

3. **Prairie Tool Company (Aberdeen) — mold builder + molder for medical devices without ISO 13485:** Prairie Tool builds production molds in P20, S7, H13, MoldMAX with EDM and high-speed machining, and also does injection molding for medical device customers. No ISO 13485 was found. Is a 15-person mold builder/molder in Aberdeen, SD realistic for Alliance engagement at 1,400+ miles from PSB?

4. **Statewide absence of RJG/cavity pressure monitoring:** Zero confirmed RJG installations across all SD injection molders. Scientific molding was confirmed only at Falcon Plastics. Does this represent a market too early in its technology adoption curve for the Alliance, or is it an opportunity to establish Aquila as the first mover?

5. **Cardinal Industries (Aberdeen) — 62-year-old plastic AND rubber injection molder with in-house tool & die:** Dual-material capability (plastic + rubber) is unusual. Does rubber injection molding create different data/tooling challenges that Aquila's platform addresses, or is it out of scope?

## SUMMARY: SOUTH DAKOTA YIELD

South Dakota outperformed expectations for a 12-CWP-row state. The beyond-CWP sweep was critical — it surfaced Royal Plastics, Cardinal Industries, Prairie Tool, and the entire Aberdeen cluster, none of which appeared in the CWP database. Falcon Plastics alone justifies the state's analysis: a $50–80M+, 152-press operation with scientific molding, aggressive automation, active M&A, in-house tooling, and 50 years of legacy data, led by a CEO already in the CWP database with 4 contacts.

The most actionable finding is the statewide certification and technology gap. SD's injection molders serve demanding markets (medical, automotive, defense) without the certifications or process monitoring infrastructure those markets increasingly require. This gap is the Alliance's entry point — and Falcon Plastics, with its scale, automation ambitions, and university relationships, is the door.
