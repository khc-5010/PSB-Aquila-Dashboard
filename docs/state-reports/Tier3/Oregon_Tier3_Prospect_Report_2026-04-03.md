## QUESTIONS FOR BRETT

1. **Metal injection molding (MIM) scope:** OptiMIM Portland (formerly Kinetics/Dynacast) is a metal injection molding facility under Form Technologies / Ares Management. MIM shares process fundamentals with plastic injection molding (feedstock pelletization, injection into mold cavities, temperature/pressure optimization) but uses metal-polymer feedstocks. Is MIM formally in scope for the Aquila platform, or only thermoplastic/thermoset injection molding? OptiMIM's process monitoring sophistication (PCpk predictive process control, closed-loop microprocessor molding) makes it technically interesting regardless.

2. **Piller Aimmco cross-border treatment:** Piller Aimmco was already profiled as WA HP #2 (Washougal, WA molding + Woodland, WA tooling). Their operational footprint straddles the OR/WA border in Portland metro. Should they be treated as a single WA prospect (per original scoring) or flagged as a Portland metro cluster anchor for combined OR/WA engagement planning? Their wireless process monitoring + digital work instructions + 35-person tooling shop makes them the most AI-ready operation in the Pacific Northwest.

3. **OMEP as channel partner:** Oregon Manufacturing Extension Partnership (OMEP) now explicitly offers AI tools for manufacturing environments. They have offices in Portland, Bend, Medford, Salem, and Albany with deep connections to the state's manufacturing base. Worth pursuing as a referral/channel partner similar to how NIST MEP centers operate in other states?

4. **Greenfield AI opportunity vs. infrastructure gap:** Oregon has zero confirmed RJG adopters and no cavity pressure monitoring installations among plastic injection molders. Does the alliance see this as (a) a harder sell requiring foundational sensor infrastructure first, or (b) an opportunity to deploy Aquila's platform as the first-mover AI solution without competing against incumbent analytics tools?

5. **Family succession as engagement trigger:** Multiple Oregon molders face imminent generational transitions — 3D Plastics (Lee Jr. retired 2021, Kelly → Elliott transition underway), Acue-Tex (founder Michael Terrell, 40+ years, 14-employee shop), Plastech (Al Hendrickson founded 1970, son Michael bought 1996). Should succession planning be elevated as a prospecting signal for knowledge capture / institutional memory preservation use cases?

## KEY INTELLIGENCE SIGNALS

### Oregon Plastics Landscape Characteristics
- **Technology desert for process monitoring:** Zero confirmed RJG, cavity pressure monitoring, or scientific molding practitioners among Oregon's plastic injection molders. Piller Aimmco (WA, but Portland metro) is the only operation with confirmed digital work instructions and wireless process monitoring. This is the most significant technology gap identified in any Tier 3 state since West Virginia.
- **Certification gap:** No Oregon plastic injection molder holds ISO 13485 (medical) or IATF 16949 (automotive). Only R&D Plastics holds AS9100D (aerospace). OptiMIM holds all three but is MIM, not plastic.
- **Family ownership dominance:** Nearly every independent Oregon molder is family-owned, many with active or imminent generational transitions: 3D Plastics (Dundas, 3rd gen), Falcon Plastics (Barlund, 3rd gen), Acue-Tex (Terrell, founder still active), Plastech (Hendrickson, 2nd gen), Nelson Unique Tooling (est. 1965), Anderson Die (est. 1954). This creates a concentrated knowledge capture opportunity across the state.
- **Portland metro concentration:** ~80% of Oregon's injection molding capacity is within 30 miles of downtown Portland, primarily in the Wilsonville–Tigard–Hillsboro–Clackamas arc. This concentration enables efficient multi-prospect engagement.
- **Minimal PE activity:** Beyond Falcon Plastics' 2019 Poly-Cast acquisition, no PE-backed injection molding platforms operate in Oregon. The state's family-owned shops represent an un-consolidated market — PE activity could accelerate rapidly if a platform acquires a beachhead.
